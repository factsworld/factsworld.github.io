🌐 Module 2: Understanding Wi-Fi Basics

Welcome back, hacker-in-training! Before we dive into the fun stuff like packet sniffing and handshakes, you need to speak the language of Wi-Fi — and trust me, it's not just shouting "Why-Fi no work?" at your router.

📡 SSID, BSSID, and MAC Addresses

  • SSID (Service Set Identifier): The fancy name for your Wi-Fi network name. You know, the one you named "FBI Surveillance Van" just to mess with your neighbors.
  • BSSID (Basic Service Set Identifier): This is the MAC address (hardware address) of the Wi-Fi access point. It’s unique to every router/AP.
  • MAC Address: A 48-bit identifier for a network interface. Looks like 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E. Like your device’s digital fingerprint.

📶 Channels and Frequencies

Wi-Fi runs on different frequencies (think: roads) and channels (think: lanes on those roads).

  • 2.4 GHz: Longer range, slower speed, more crowded (like a free public beach)
  • 5 GHz: Shorter range, faster speed, more channels = less interference
  • 6 GHz (Wi-Fi 6E): The new hotshot — faster, cleaner, and future-proof
🎯 Real-world tip: When hacking, choose a less crowded channel. Tools like airodump-ng show channel numbers. Stick to channels 1, 6, or 11 on 2.4GHz — they don’t overlap.

🔒 Wi-Fi Security Protocols

Time to meet the Wi-Fi security family — the good, the bad, and the obsolete:

  • WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy): More like Worthless Encryption Protocol. Can be cracked in minutes. Used only in museums and old coffee machines.
  • WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access): A step up, but still vulnerable. Uses TKIP which is now considered weak.
  • WPA2: The standard for over a decade. Secure if using strong passwords.
  • WPA3: The latest and greatest, but not widely adopted yet. Harder to crack, but still researchable!

🧪 How Wi-Fi Communication Works

Let’s simplify it:

  1. Your device says, “Hey router, are you there?”
  2. The router replies, “Yep, let’s connect. Here’s some info.”
  3. You handshake (negotiate encryption), and voilà — Netflix starts buffering again.

This handshake process is where hacking magic happens — we can capture and analyze these packets to extract passwords or impersonate access points. But more on that later 😉

💡 Fun Fact: You don’t need to be connected to a network to see its handshake. Just listen at the right time (like a digital ninja).

✅ Summary

  • SSID is the name, BSSID is the MAC of the access point
  • 2.4GHz = long range, 5GHz = fast & clean
  • WEP is trash, WPA2 is decent, WPA3 is new kid on the block
  • We’ll use the handshake process to break into networks (ethically!)
