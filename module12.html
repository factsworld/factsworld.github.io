Module 12: WPS Attacks

🔓 Module 12: WPS Attacks – Breaking the Push Button Party

WPS stands for Wi-Fi Protected Setup. It's that magical button on your router that was meant to help grandma connect her printer... but accidentally left the front door wide open for hackers. 🧓📶

🔍 What Is WPS?

It’s a feature that allows devices to connect with the press of a button or by entering an 8-digit PIN. Guess what? That PIN is bruteforceable. And routers don’t even lock out after 1000 tries. 😬

🛠️ Tools Needed

  • airmon-ng – For monitor mode
  • wash – Scan for WPS-enabled networks
  • reaver – The star of the show (PIN bruteforcer)

📡 Scan for WPS Networks

wash -i wlan0mon

This will list nearby networks with WPS enabled. Look for ones that say WPS Locked: No.

💣 Launching the Attack

reaver -i wlan0mon -b [BSSID] -vv

This command will start bruteforcing the WPS PIN. -vv gives verbose output so you can enjoy the show. 🍿

⏱️ How Long It Takes?

Usually 2–8 hours depending on the router and signal strength. So… set it up, grab coffee, and come back later. ☕

🎁 Result?

If successful, Reaver will give you the WPA PSK (the actual Wi-Fi password). From there, you’re in!

🚨 Real-World Reminder

💡 Only a few routers today still have WPS enabled and vulnerable. But it still exists, especially on older routers or default setups. Look for routers with no rate limiting and party on! 🎉

🛑 Legal Tip

Only test WPS attacks on your own network or authorized test labs. Never try this at your neighbor's house, even if their Wi-Fi is called 'FreeNet'. 🍕

📌 Summary

  • WPS can be bruteforced using tools like Reaver
  • WPS PIN is only 8 digits and easily guessed
  • Great for testing security of old routers
